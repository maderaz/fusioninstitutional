Not all vaults are created equal.
Most people find out too late.

DC
Darren Camas
Co-Founder, IPOR Labs · I Build Vaults for a Living

I've been building DeFi infrastructure for 5+ years alongside Dimitar Dinev and the IPOR team. Over the past ~15 months, we've taken Fusion from $0 to $40M TVL. Along the way, I've had dozens of conversations with funds, onchain allocators, integrators, and partners — and a few patterns have become impossible to ignore.

This is not a whitepaper. It's a collection of personal observations about why vaults are becoming the central piece of DeFi infrastructure — and why the design choices underneath them matter far more than most people realize.

Vaults are the 2026 meta — but not all vaults mean the same thing

Everyone is talking about vaults going into 2026. That part is broadly correct. The curator sector continues to grow across TVL, users, interfaces, and tooling — from marginal adoption to over $10B TVL at peak since 2023.

The first wave was pioneered by protocols like Yearn back in 2022, in an environment defined by triple-digit APYs and strategies centered around rewards autocompounding. As vault adoption grew, vaults stopped being just yield wrappers and started evolving into infrastructure.

That shift accelerated with the rise of the curator economy — pushed into the spotlight by Morpho through Risk Curators and MetaMorpho Managers. For the first time, vaults stopped being built primarily for end users and started being built for curators: entities responsible for allocation, execution, and risk across multiple markets on behalf of others.

This is where the line between DeFi and TradFi starts to blur. The curator role maps closely to functions traditionally handled by brokers, portfolio managers, and asset managers — but executed natively onchain. Vaults stop being consumer products and start behaving like institutional infrastructure: allocation surfaces, execution engines, and accounting systems rolled into one.

Trust-me-bro vs. transparent vault setups

2025 set the stage and exposed weaknesses. A year primarily driven by macro ended with a daisy chain of events linked to CEX deleveraging — which sent shockwaves through a project called Stream Finance, its "stablecoin" xUSD (a vault at root level), and contagion to related markets.

The main culprit? Trust. Net Asset Value manually fed from offchain. Opacity and manual calculation left depositors feeling rich, with a gaping hole on the other side and zero transparency on which market participants could act.

If we replace Stream's xUSD with any RWA — it becomes a collateral asset looking for yield. Many vault products are now built to optimize this yield and turn it into a consumer-facing product. If 2026 is a boon for RWA and Vaults, this is a match made in heaven or hell.

The more the infrastructure layer itself is "trust-me-bro" — from yield to NAV calculation to automation and interface — the more blowups we'll have. The stronger the embrace of trust-minimized infrastructure, real-time accounting, full automation and risk management, the greater the influx of capital into DeFi.

Where the capital will flow

The complexity of onchain finance is increasing. Lending, borrowing, looping, hedging, and arbitrage no longer happen inside a single protocol or even a single ecosystem. They span multiple markets, risk parameters, oracle assumptions, and liquidity conditions.

What used to be a single interaction has become a sequence of dependent actions. Each step introduces friction. Each interaction introduces execution risk. Each protocol boundary becomes a potential failure point.

The alternative is obvious. Vaults.

Vaults that automate complex executions, with defined constraints, continuous accounting, and integrated risk management. The vault stops being a wrapper and becomes the execution surface.

For depositors — whether a wallet user, a CEX depositor, a family office, or a hedge fund — vaults become a single interface. One point of entry. Yield accrues as a vault share price rather than through fragmented cash flows. Accounting becomes continuous. Exposure is expressed through ownership rather than a sequence of actions.

In 2026, capital will flow toward vaults that make complexity operable — with automated execution, continuous accounting, and verifiable risk.

A word on vault accounting

Vaults orchestrate liquidity, coordinate execution, and act as intelligence and risk-management layers on top of DeFi. At that point, accounting becomes paramount.

When I analyze vault infrastructure, I look for a few non-negotiables:

  • The system should be permissionless: anyone should be able to deploy a vault from an audited, verifiable factory.
  • Its limitations and guarantees should be transparent and observable onchain.
  • Administrative roles must be explicit: who can do what, under which constraints, and with what safeguards.
  • There must be a single interface where all of this can be understood without reading smart contracts.
  • Asset and position tracking should be straightforward: if funds and yield are onchain, showing where the money is should not be optional.

Accounting should be trust-minimized, transparent, real-time, and onchain. The more leniency you allow around share price calculation, the more you implicitly rely on trust rather than verification.

Why we built Fusion

I'm not neutral here. I build vault infrastructure at IPOR Labs, and Fusion reflects many of the principles discussed above: trust-minimized accounting, automation-first execution, explicit permissions, and transparent state.

As vaults become the interface between TradFi capital and DeFi markets, infrastructure quality — not yield — will decide where trust and capital ultimately settle.

That's what we're building for.
